How I got my visa: all the pitfalls

Last modified 2025-10-25.

I ran into a lot of issues when applying for my visa, and I felt the process could have been better documented. This article aims to fill in some gaps. To keep things coherent, I'll go chronologically through what I did and what I discovered. Naturally, that will not result in a chronological account of what I should have done, but it will at least help you plan your application to be more efficient than mine.

0. Background (with important info!)

In early 2024 I decided to leave the UK. I told my employer about that decision and asked to continue my work remotely, since I liked my job and we already had remote workers. They agreed, but since they didn't have a presence in the Czech Republic, I could not be an employee there. Instead, we agreed we'd end my employment before I moved, and I would offer consultancy services as an independent contractor.

In August 2024 I started my hunt for a Czech digital nomad visa. If you're reading this page, I'm assuming you're trying to apply for the digital nomad scheme (or a Czech business visa) yourself, so I won't go into what exactly the scheme is. I was eligible because I'm a UK citizen, I work as a software engineer for >1.5x the average Czech salary, and I have a STEM degree.

The best documentation I found for the process was on the Seoul embassy's page. There are two streams: employee and freelancer. Because of the arrangements I described above, I chose freelancer. I don't know the details of the employee stream; if you're on it, I hope most of what's below still helps you.

On the page I shared above, there is a link to another page from the Czech Ministry of Industry and Trade which has some more documents, listed at the bottom of the page. I read

  • Digital Nomad Program (pdf),
  • Step by step - freelancer (docx), and
  • FAQ - Digital Nomad Program (docx).

The others were either related to employee applicants, or were not relevant yet. The most important document was 'Step by step - freelancer'. It lists seven steps, but what you'll need to do for this visa falls more naturally into four steps:

  1. Set up a business in the Czech Republic
  2. Apply to the digital nomad program
  3. Apply for a visa
  4. Finalise your business application

1. The nightmarish first step

This first step made all the others look easy. My pet theory is this: the Czech government departments I was dealing with on the other steps - the London embassy and the digital nomad program - were used to helping clueless foreigners such as myself, but on this step, I was dealing with a municipal trade licencing office in Prague, so I was fairly expected to act like a Czech. But I'm not a Czech, and neither are you, so I hope this section saves you all the time I wasted.

For a freelancer applicant, you need to register a trade. If you're applying to the digital nomad program, you'll be offering services in IT or marketing, which are among the 'unqualified' trades. That simplifies things, since you don't need to prove to the trade office that you're qualified to offer these services, unlike, say, if you were a doctor or a lawyer.

The easiest way to register is using the online portal. Some useful background info can be found here, from the Ministry of Industry and Trade. You have to digitally sign your application, either by sending it with some Czech government ID, or with a downloadable app they offer. As far as I can tell, the app doesn't work. So you need to get your hands on some Czech government ID.

1.1. The first pitfall: databoxes and Czech POINTs

In the Czech Republic, you often need to verify your identity online, especially when communicating with the government. It's quite a user-friendly, joined-up system, which is very helpful once you're actually signed up to it. The difficult part is getting signed up outside of the Czech Republic. There are three main ways of getting verified online:

  1. Your Czech online banking account will often be usable for this.
  2. MojeID, which seems to be the main one, and a little more powerful than the others.
  3. A databox, or datov치 schr치nka

To get a Czech bank account, you need to prove you're allowed to live in the Czech Republic. Well, you're not allowed yet, so that option is out. MojeID seems to be more for Czech citizens. There may be a way to get one if you're a foreign national, similar to how you get a databox, but I don't know. I used a databox.

A databox is like a secure messaging service where you can send and receive documents. You can also use your databox login to sign up to other secure online services, which is what we need it for at the moment. It's worth warning you that documents get deleted after 30 days unless you pay for storage, so if you don't pay, make sure you're downloading everything. Like the other methods, you'll need to verify your identity to open a databox, and there's a way for foreigners to do this: the Czech POINT.

What is a Czech POINT?

A Czech POINT is a physical location, the vast majority of which are in the Czech Republic, where you can communicate with the Czech government. They are usually found in Czech post offices. There are two in the UK, one in the Czech embassy in London, the other in the consulate in Manchester. Your country hopefully has one or two as well. There's a list linked at the end of this page. You don't need an appointment if you go to a Czech post office, but your embassy or consulate might require one. Even if they have non-appointment times, these are likely to be a couple of hours a week and may not be advertised anywhere, so make sure to email them and find out.

Bring your passport to the Czech POINT (this is all about verifying your identity, remember!) and ask to set up a databox. It's polite to have memorised the Czech name 'datov치 schr치nka' too, in case you're not speaking Czech and they don't know what you mean by 'databox'. They'll ask for some basic information to help you set up the account.

There was a deeply annoying sub-pitfall here for me. The first-time login details have to be sent to you by secure post, either by the Czech postal service making you sign for the letter with ID, or with your country's equivalent service. The UK has no such service, so the letter I actually received simply told me to return to the Czech POINT to reset my login details. You may be able to avoid this fate if you live in a country with a postal service superior to Royal Mail, or if you can explain this potential problem at your initial Czech POINT appointment. In fact, the databox website's Czech POINT sign-up instructions say that the login details can be sent to your email, so I don't know for sure that any of that had to happen. It's worth making you aware, just in case.

A databox is useful for more than just verifying your identity. With a Czech POINT, it also allows you to convert between electronic and paper copies of documents, where each copy so obtained has the same legal authority as an original (unless it's a non-copyable document like a passport). This is important, because you'll need to submit originals of various documents throughout this process. To get a paper document converted to electronic, bring it to a Czech POINT, explain what you want, and give them your databox ID. It's worth also bringing physical ID1 just in case. To get an electronic document printed, upload the original .zfo2 file here, print the page with the barcode, and bring it to the Czech POINT.

1 In the Czech Republic, ID means your passport or an EU ID card. If you're reading this, you're probably not an EU national, so it means your passport. Your country's national ID card may also be fine, but your passport definitely is. In the UK, we don't have ID cards, so we tend to use driving licences if we need to be IDed. The post office might accept your driving licence, but they are within their rights to turn it down.

2 A .zfo is the file type you'll download from databox data messages. It contains the actual document (like a PDF) as well as metadata verifying the document. Just giving the post office a PDF doesn't tell them that the the document is genuine, so they won't be able to convert it for you.

Back to business. Now you have your databox, you can use it to send off your trade application. In your application you'll be asked which municipal trade office you want to use. Pick one close to where you'll be living, because you'll be going there a few days after you arrive in the country. The documents you need are exactly the ones they list: application form, proof you've paid the fee, proof you have no criminal record, proof you have permission to use your registered office, and so on. They also say you need to prove you're a resident of the Czech Republic. That's apparently a catch-22, because you're not, and you won't be unless you register this trade. There also doesn't seem to be a separate form for making provisional registrations. What actually happens is you submit the rest of the application, and they issue a 'provisional extract from the trade register', which you use for the rest of your visa application. When you've got the visa, you go back to the trade office, show them the visa, and finalise the application. More on that in section 4. For now, there are some more fun pitfalls.

1.2. ACROs, apostilles, and registered offices

You may already have some rented accommodation ready in the Czech Republic. You can use that as your registered office if you have the landlord's permission, but just providing your lease contract to the trade office may not be sufficient proof of this permission. For the Prague 3 trade office, it wasn't. They had a separate form to be completed by the landlord. You'll need to send it to the trade office by post with your case number, or get the original converted to electronic form (see pitfall 1.1) to submit it via databox.

The other option is to rent a virtual office. There are sites where you pay a small monthly fee and immediately have access to a permission letter. This is faster and more convenient, but I have nothing to say about the experience of using them, since I got permission from my landlord. When I was talking to the Prague 3 trade office, they recommended I use these services on account of their speed. Your trade office may be able to recommend a specific company. Whoever you use, make sure that any post arriving at the virtual address finds its way to you.

You'll also need to prove to the trade office that you have no criminal record. There are two reasons I bring this up.

Firstly, your country may have two different criminal record checks depending on whether it's for internal use or visa use. For example, the UK has DBS checks for internal use, but to get a visa, you obtain a different kind of check called an ACRO. The trade office may not care which kind you submit, but if you get an ACRO now you can reuse it for your visa application, and not have to pay for a DBS check.

Of course, this record will need to be translated into Czech. If you're also using this translation for your visa, it will need to be from an approved translator. I can recommend Absolute Czech Translations if you're in the UK. Again, the trade office may not care whether your translation is a proper visa-style one, but it's worth doing.

Secondly, your foreign government documents have no legal power in the Czech Republic. You need to certify them for international use, using a verification process known as an apostille. I can recommend Hague Apostille Service if you're in the UK. Get your documents apostilled and then translated, because the apostille certificate will need to be translated too.

Now that you have your documents in order, you can send them off. You'll need to either post originals (or authorised copies) to the trade office, or get the originals converted in electronic form at a Czech POINT and submit them via the databox. You may not be able to add all these documents to the online registration form. That's fine - just complete the questions in the form, submit it, and the trade office will send you a scary databox message asking you to fix the gaps in your application. You can reply with your documents.

As far as I can tell, there's also no way to pay the application fee on the form. Instead, the 'fix your application' message will tell you you haven't paid, and instruct you to send them a bank transfer. They'll tell you what to write in the reference so they know it's from you. But they'll only give you the Czech internal bank details, not an international bank account number. That means you can't do the transfer from your foreign bank account (unless your bank is a lot more helpful than mine). I used Wise to do the transfer, and to make sure the trade office knew I'd sent it, I sent them the transfer receipt.

Once they have all these documents, they should issue your provisional trade licence within five working days. You'll then have six months to finalise the application. Be aware that you may run into roadblocks I haven't encountered here; each licencing office may have different requirements.

2. The easy bit, with a couple of caveats

For me, the previous step was by far the hardest. If I stop this article here I'll have done my duty. However, there are a few more things I can help with.

Once you have your trade licence, you can fill in the application form and email it to the digital nomad program with all the other stuff: proof of education or experience, proof you have an employment or consultancy contract signed, and so on. It's all listed in the links above. If there are issues they tend to get back to you quickly and explain things well. Also, you can submit documents to them in English, and they don't need to be in special digitally signed formats like .zfo.

There's just one problem: they're too fast! They'll give you a visa interview appointment which will be in the very near future, and if you don't have everything ready for it, you won't have time to get it ready before the interview. You can reschedule it once, but only once, and the new date they give you may not be a convenient one. Save yourself the trouble and get everything ready before you send the application to the digital nomad program.

3. Not hard either, but no second chances

Up until now, every step in the process has allowed you to fix your mistakes if you make an incorrect submission. Your visa interview is not so flexible: you have to get everything right on the day. There are a couple of government websites which list what you need:

  • For example the Ministry of the Interior, (this link is specifically for business visas)
  • or the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (this link is for general long-term visas).

They both list essentially the same things, some of which I'll discuss below. Keep in mind every document you submit needs to be under 180 days old, apart from passports, birth certificates, diplomas and the like.

Most documents can be submitted in certified copies (made at a Czech POINT). Of course, that does not apply to the non-copyable documents I discussed above, nor to documents supporting the purpose of stay. That means that if you're submitting an employment or consultancy contract, it needs to be an original. If you're a freelancer, submit your contract as well as your provisional trade licence. Your embassy may allow you to save money on the translations for the contract by only translating the first few pages, as long as they have the essential details: the parties' names, your singatures, the details of the work and pay, et cetera. Make sure you confirm first with your embassy that this will be acceptable.

For proof of your financial means, you may also be able to get a partial translation of a bank statement which includes opening and closing balances, dates, and your name. This should be cheaper than translating each individual transaction, which seems a bit pointless. Absolute Czech Translations offered this when I used their services.

Your photos have to be Schengen visa compliant. If you have a passport photo machine near you, they should have the option to print the correct dimensions for a Schengen visa. Many post offices also offer this service.

Your proof of accommodation is usually pretty self-explanatory; just make sure it covers the whole period of the visa you've applied for. If you can, it's worth making a Czech POINT conversion of your rental contract into electronic format, because the original will be kept on file by the Czech government, but it may be useful later in the Czech Republic as a proof of address.

Your proof of no criminal record should be an officially translated, apostilled ACRO if you're in the UK, not a DBS check. This is the issue I discussed in Pitfall 1.2. Your country may have an equivalent international criminal record check.

The other required documents are simple and I have no helpful advice for them. Just make sure you've double- and triple-checked the requirements once you have all your documents ready, to make sure you haven't missed anything.

3.1. Medical insurance

When you pick up your visa, you'll need to show that you have medical insurance covering your whole stay. It needs to be compliant with act 326/1999 on the residence of foreigners in the Czech Republic. This means that, among other requirements, the insurance must cover harm caused to you by your own intoxication, and harm caused to yourself intentionally. I could not find any insurance which actually seemed to cover these. However, plenty of insurance products (like PZVP Plus or Exclusive), claim to be fully compliant with the act. Mine, one of Maxima's products, states on the insurance certificates that it is fully compliant. Maybe there are fine points in the insurance terms or in act 326/1999 which I don't fully understand; I'm not a lawyer. All this is to say, don't panic if you can't find health insurance which you're sure covers intoxication and self-harm. Just make sure the insurance company states that their product is compliant with the act.

Another potentially helpful point is that the embassy may accept your GHIC card (or national equivalent). If you want to try that, make sure you ask them beforehand!

4. Getting to the Czech Republic

Once you get to the Czech Republic, you need to visit the Foreign Police to register your arrival, then finalise your trade licence application by bringing the trade office your visa and your provisional registration. It needs to be the same municipal trade office which you used for your application. Make sure you bring evidence of your medical insurance to both appointments just in case. Once you've registered your trade fully, you'll have an I캛O. This is the registration number for your business. You should receive a letter from the social security administration within eight days explaining how to pay contributions, and you'll need to contact a tax office to set up income tax. Your trade office can advise you as to whether you're obligated to sign up to VZP, the state medical insurer. If you do sign up, let your private insurance know this is why you're cancelling, and they should issue a partial refund (prorated for the time you've been insured) if you haven't made a claim yet.

4.1. Strange stamps

This is a final pitfall which hopefully doesn't apply to you. My trade office weren't satisfied with just my visa and asked that I obtain a 'pobytov칠 raz칤tko' (residence stamp) in my passport. This is a stamp from the Ministry of the Interior showing my address in the Czech Republic, and can be obtained at the office at 콯ukovsk칠ho 888/2, 161 00 Praha 6-Ruzyn캩.

I'd never heard of this stamp and certainly hadn't seen it on any trade licencing requirements. The staff at the ministry were just as confused as me. But they gave me the stamp, after which the trade office issued my licence.

After I got the trade licence, I received a letter asking for 2690 CZK to appear in a third-party commercial register. You don't have to respond.

Epilogue

This is only my experience. I may have made mistakes, and you unfortunately may find your own issues which I haven't run into. If any of what I've said contradicts official documentation, please believe the documentation and not me. As I said above, this article just aims to fill in some gaps.

This is as far as I've got myself. I'm writing this on 13 June and I got to Prague on 1 June, so I'm still awaiting my tax registrations. There may be more administrative difficulties I discover, so I'll document those in another post if they come up.

Good luck, and enjoy life in the Czech Republic!

An update: tax for freelancers

I don't know how employees are supposed to pay tax; this section may only be helpful to sole traders. There are three taxes I needed to worry about, which the trade office may mention to you: income tax, social security, and health insurance. As I said, health insurance may not apply to you if you have private foreigners' insurance. For income and social security, you need to sign up yourself.

My partner had previously registered as a sole trader and received his social security registration letter 20 days later without manually signing up. He also contacted the local tax office by email with his I캛O and received his DI캛, the income tax registration number. I took the same approach - emailing the tax office - to no avail.

I did get something else in the post, though: a letter telling me my databox was ready for activation. Strange, since I already have one. Still, I activated this different databox, and waiting for me was an extract from the register of persons, recognising me as a foreign sole trader. That looked promising - an online identifier associated with my sole trader registration. I used this new databox to sign into the online tax service, MOJE dan캩, and sure enough, a DI캛 was waiting for me on the landing page.

For social security it was a bit more manual: you need to notify them via a form and send it via your databox. They'll respond with a letter telling you how much you need to pay in advance (which, if you haven't paid Czech taxes before, will probably be nothing).

That's it for now. Remember, all the information here comes from my personal experience, and I'm only one person. If you're relying on a piece of information you've found here, please verify it via an official source.
